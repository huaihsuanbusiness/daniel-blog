## The Disillusionment Phase

Every transformative technology goes through a similar pattern: excitement, peak hype, disillusionment, and then — eventually — real productivity. AI is firmly in the disillusionment phase now, and that's actually a good thing.

The hype was deafening. Every company became an "AI company." Every product had a GPT wrapper. Venture capital flowed into projects that had nothing more than a chatbot and a dream.

## What's Actually Working

Let's be specific. Where is AI delivering real value today?

**Code generation and assistance**
This is the clearest win. AI coding tools have genuinely increased developer productivity — not by replacing developers, but by handling the boilerplate, the repetitive tasks, the documentation that slows human thinking.

**Content summarization and synthesis**
Reading 50 articles to understand a new field? AI can synthesize that into 5 key insights. Not perfect, but genuinely useful for initial orientation.

**Structured data extraction**
Taking messy, unstructured information and extracting it into databases, spreadsheets, organized notes — this works well and has immediate applicability.

## What's Overblown

**Autonomous agents**
The vision of AI agents that autonomously accomplish complex multi-step tasks is still largely fantasy. The reality is narrow, brittle, and requires significant human oversight.

**Reasoning and planning**
Current LLMs are sophisticated pattern matchers, not reasoning engines. They can simulate reasoning convincingly, but they fail in ways that are predictable only in their unpredictability.

**Replacing knowledge workers wholesale**
Every serious AI deployment I've seen in enterprise still requires human experts to validate, correct, and make judgment calls. The "AI will replace X" narrative ignores how much expert work is actually judgment calls embedded in context.

## Where the Real Opportunities Are

The winners in this space aren't building AI — they're applying AI to specific, high-value workflows in industries that haven't been disrupted yet.

The key questions:
- What's the specific problem?
- What's the cost of errors?
- How much does human oversight actually cost?
- What's the total addressable market for a solution?

The boring answer is usually: pick one vertical, understand it deeply, build the AI wrapper around domain expertise, and charge accordingly.

## The Next 18 Months

My prediction: we'll see continued improvement in reasoning capabilities, but the biggest value will come from better deployment of what we already have. Integration costs are still high. Context windows are still limited. Reliability is still a challenge.

The companies that win won't be the ones with the best model. They'll be the ones that figure out how to make AI actually work in production, for real users, with real consequences for failure.

That's a harder problem than it looks.
